The Implicit Bargain (1995-2022)

▼

For 25 years, the internet ran on an implicit deal: users get free content, creators get traffic, platforms get ad revenue. Google Search was the switchboard—routing attention to publishers who monetized it with ads.

This created a $600B+ digital advertising economy. Publishers invested in content because traffic = revenue. The more you were cited, linked, and visited, the more you earned.

Peak newspaper ad revenue
$49.4B (2005)

LLMs break this bargain. When the answer appears in the search box, there's no click. No visit. No ad impression. The content is consumed, but the creator isn't compensated.

The Zero-Click Trap

▼

When AI summaries appear in search results, click-through rates collapse. Users get what they need without visiting the source.

Zero-click rate with AI Overviews
83%

For every 1,000 searches in the US, only 360 clicks go to the open web. The rest stay within Google's ecosystem or end with no click at all.

This is efficient for users but catastrophic for the economic model that funded content creation. The information is extracted; the value isn't returned.

Source: SparkToro Zero-Click Study 2024

The Chegg Canary

▼

Chegg was an online education company worth $15 billion at its peak. Students paid for homework help and tutoring services. Then ChatGPT launched.

Chegg market cap decline
-99% ($15B → $156M)

Students discovered they could ask ChatGPT the same questions for free. Chegg's subscriber base collapsed. They laid off 45% of their workforce and are now exploring a sale or going private.

Chegg is the canary in the coal mine. Any business that answers questions for money is vulnerable: reference sites, how-to guides, educational platforms, Q&A services.

The pattern: if your value proposition is "we have the answer," and AI can provide that answer instantly and freely, your business model is obsolete.

Model Collapse: The Recursive Trap

▼

A 2024 Nature study demonstrated that AI models trained on AI-generated content progressively degrade. Each generation loses information about edge cases and minority data until the model produces nonsense.

AI-generated content (new web pages)
74.2%

If most new content is AI-generated, and AI companies scrape the web for training data, they're increasingly training on their own outputs. The researchers showed that after just a few generations, a model prompted about "medieval architecture" started outputting text about "jackrabbits."

Human-generated content isn't just nice to have—it's the substrate that keeps AI useful. Defunding human content creation is, ironically, degrading the AI that replaced it.

Source: Nature - AI models collapse when trained on recursively generated data

The Stack Overflow Paradox

▼

Stack Overflow was the backbone of developer knowledge for 15 years. Millions of programmers asked questions, and the community answered. This corpus became training data for AI coding assistants.

Stack Overflow questions decline (post-ChatGPT)
-76%

Now developers ask AI instead of Stack Overflow. The questions aren't being asked publicly. The answers aren't being refined by the community. Edge cases aren't being documented.

This is the paradox: the AI learned from Stack Overflow, replaced Stack Overflow, and is now degrading the knowledge base it needs for future improvement. The community-driven knowledge commons is being privatized and hollowed out simultaneously.

Source: Eric Holscher - Stack Overflow's decline

Who Pays for Training Data?

▼

AI companies generated ~$15B+ in ARR in 2025, largely from models trained on scraped content. Total licensing payments to content creators: ~$500M. The ratio speaks for itself.

Value extraction ratio
30:1 (ARR to licensing)

There are 40+ pending lawsuits over AI training data. The US Copyright Office ruled that using "vast troves" of copyrighted works for competing commercial products "goes beyond established fair use boundaries."

The emerging licensing market (Reddit: $203M, News Corp: $250M, HarperCollins: $5,000/book) is creating precedent. If there's a market price for training data, the fair use defense weakens.

The question isn't whether AI should exist. It's whether the people who created the content that makes AI valuable should be compensated—and if so, how.

The Reddit Play

▼

Reddit recognized early that its user-generated content was valuable training data. Instead of fighting AI companies, they licensed it—$203M in deals with Google, OpenAI, and others.

Reddit data licensing revenue
$203M (2-3 year contracts)

This represents a new business model: if you can't beat the AI companies, sell to them. Reddit blocked unauthorized crawlers and required licensing agreements. Their IPO timing was impeccable.

The catch: Reddit's users created that content for free, for the community. Now it's being monetized by Reddit, not the creators. This tension—who owns user-generated content—is the next frontier of the AI economy debate.

Source: Columbia Journalism Review
